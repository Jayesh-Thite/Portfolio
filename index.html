Score Simulator Experian Credit App

What if your users could test a financial decision before they make it?

We built a credit score simulator — from zero — with no existing mobile pattern to reference, for a product that had to feel effortless despite modelling eight distinct financial scenarios.

The Problem

People know their score. Nobody tells them what it actually responds to.

Who they are

Credit-aware Indians on the Experian app

Salaried users, credit card holders, loan borrowers. Financially literate — but score-illiterate. They track their number without understanding what drives it.

What they're trying to do

Make a smart credit decision — without breaking something

Should I take a new loan? Pay off this card first? What if I miss one EMI? Users want to understand the impact of a decision before they make it.

Where they're stuck

The app answers "what." Never "what if."

The product shows a score and its history. But there's no sandbox to test decisions in. A score dip sends users into guesswork. The gap between seeing a number and knowing what to do with it is entirely unaddressed.

Why the business is solving this

A simulator that turns curious users into confident ones — and confident ones into paying ones

If a user can see that "closing this tradeline drops your score by 34 points," they're far more likely to act on a recommendation, engage with premium features, and trust the product enough to convert. The simulator isn't just educational — it's a retention and conversion lever built on top of a real utility.

Discovery

We looked for a reference. There wasn't one.

Before opening Figma, we ran a competitive audit across the credit and personal finance landscape. The goal was simple: find a mobile pattern that handled multi-scenario simulation, understand how it worked, and figure out how to do it better.

There was no pattern to find. Only a single competitor offered anything close — and it lived exclusively on web. No app had solved this on mobile. We weren't adapting a known interaction model. We were the first to define what it even looks like on a phone screen.

"There was no design to look at and say we'd do better than this. We had to decide what better even meant."

That was both the pressure and the opportunity. Every structural decision — information hierarchy, input model, score reveal, multi-select behaviour — was ours to set from scratch. Whatever shipped would become the bar others reference next.

Scope

Eight scenarios. One simulator. Zero room for complexity to show.

The product team defined eight simulation scenarios — each modelling a real financial behaviour and its projected credit impact. Some operated at the portfolio level, affecting the full credit profile. Others targeted individual tradelines, like a specific loan or card account.

Ideation

Two designers. Parallel directions. One client decision.

We ran a parallel ideation sprint — each designer independently developed a full wireframe variation of the simulator. The intent wasn't to compete; it was to surface fundamentally different structural approaches, so the decision we took to the client was reasoned, not arbitrary.

System Thinking

Three principles anchored both directions at the product level — regardless of what the individual screens looked like:

Principle 01

Reduce cognitive load

The simulator does the heavy lifting. The UI surfaces only what the user needs at each step — nothing more.

Principle 02

Reversible by default

Selecting a scenario should feel as light as deselecting it. No dead ends, no commitment anxiety.

Principle 03

Motion as feedback

Every selection updates the projected score visibly. Motion communicates change — it isn't decoration.

Screen Thinking

At the screen level, the key decision was the home layout — how users first encounter and choose scenarios. This is where the two wireframes diverged most sharply, and where the client's final call was made.

My direction was selected. The feedback pointed to two things: the scenario cards were immediately scannable, and toggling scenarios on and off felt natural without any learning curve.

UI Exploration

Three layout directions before the right one appeared

With wireframes approved, we moved to visual design. The home screen — the first thing a user encounters when they open the simulator — went through three distinct directions before we landed.

Direction 01

Dashboard-inspired grid. Felt too heavy for a single-purpose tool.

Direction 02

Large scroll cards. Room for each scenario, but the full range stayed hidden.

Selected

Direction 03

Compact, glanceable cards. All scenarios visible at once. Multi-select native to the grid.

Direction 03 solved the core tension: users needed to survey all options before committing to any. A compact card grid let them understand the full scope of the simulator before making a single tap.

Flow Architecture

Three screens, each with one job

The entire experience collapsed into three screens — no extra confirmation states, no unnecessary layers. Each screen has a single purpose and a clean handoff to the next.

"The result screen is the moment the product earns trust — by showing the user not just what changed, but why."

From Design to Build

The prototype spoke for itself. The specs made sure the build did too.

The final design included a full dark mode — not a colour flip, but a screen-by-screen pass for legibility, contrast, and component integrity across both themes. An interactive prototype captured the complete experience: disclaimer drawer, scenario toggling, animated score reveal, and loader states with contextual microcopy.

The prototype ended up being used directly by the client for internal stakeholder walkthroughs. It didn't need a walkthrough — it explained itself. A complete design spec covering interactions, component behaviour, animation timing, and edge cases ensured the engineering team's implementation decisions were obvious, not open-ended.

Metrics we'll track post-launch

Engagement

Simulator session depth

Are users running one simulation or several? Multi-scenario engagement signals the feature is working as designed, not just as a one-tap curiosity.

Retention

Return visits to the simulator

Does a user come back after their first run? Repeat usage is the clearest signal that the tool has genuine utility — not just novelty.

Conversion

CTA tap rate post-simulation

Does seeing a projected score change move users toward a recommended action? This is the direct line between the simulator and revenue.

Reflection

What this project taught me

Absence of reference is a design brief in itself

When there's no prior art, the first decision isn't what to design — it's what success even looks like. Defining that clearly before touching Figma was the most important work I did on this project.

Parallel exploration is faster than iteration

Running two independent wireframe directions simultaneously surfaced structural trade-offs in days, not weeks. The client decision was easier because the options were genuinely different — not variations of the same idea.

A prototype that explains itself is the strongest handoff

When the client used the prototype for internal sign-off without any guidance from us, that was the clearest signal the design was working. Clarity in motion is harder to achieve than clarity in static screens — and more valuable.
